01
Platform Adoption · Finastra

When shipping isn't enough

It all started when a security enhancement shipped, and barely anyone turned it on. Forty percent adoption after a major release wasn't a product failure. It was a rollout failure. And it was fixable.

25% → 93% platform adoption 40% → 70% security feature uptake

As a Product Analyst at Finastra, I noticed a recurring pattern: customers were receiving valuable capabilities without always understanding how they could benefit from them. Lenders across the US were staying on older workflows out of habit, not preference.

Before changing the rollout approach, I spent time understanding why customers weren't adopting new capabilities, reviewing feedback patterns, support conversations and discussions from customer engagements. I then redesigned how we approached rollout entirely. Instead of treating delivery as the finish line, I built a structured customer engagement programme: pre-launch walkthroughs tailored by lender size, live onboarding sessions, feedback loops built into every release, and release notes rewritten in plain language that spoke to what lenders cared about, not what engineers built.

For the security enhancement specifically, I ran targeted outreach across enterprise accounts, pairing feature walkthroughs with clear explanations of compliance risk. Within two quarters, feature adoption increased from 40% to 70%. Separately, the mobile platform reached 93% adoption through onboarding, enablement and customer engagement initiatives. I also participated in annual customer connect events, running sessions on new capabilities, gathering feedback directly from lenders and bringing those insights back into future product discussions.

What I learned: The gap between "shipped" and "adopted" is almost always a communication and trust problem, not a product problem. Solving it meant showing up throughout the entire rollout, before launch, during launch, and long after the release notes were published.

02
Regulatory Compliance · Finastra

Translating GSE mandates into platform features, without breaking anything

It all started with a regulatory change deadline and a platform serving thousands of US lenders. Missing GSE compliance wasn't an option. But neither was a rushed feature that destabilised what lenders relied on daily.

100% GSE compliance maintained 20+ regulatory initiatives delivered

As Product Analyst at Finastra, I was responsible for translating complex GSE (Government Sponsored Enterprise) regulatory changes into scalable mortgage platform enhancements, while maintaining the stability thousands of lenders depended on.

Each regulatory cycle began with deep impact analysis across integrated mortgage applications. I mapped dependencies, identified risk areas, and worked with Engineering and Architecture to define the safest implementation path, not always the fastest one.

I introduced a formal pre-release system impact review that caught integration issues before they reached production. The result: measurably fewer production incidents and stronger release stability across quarterly delivery cycles.

Over three years, we delivered 20+ regulatory and platform initiatives while maintaining 100% GSE compliance, a record that mattered deeply to lenders operating in a regulated environment where a compliance gap can halt operations.

What I learned: In regulated environments, the PM's job isn't just to ship features, it's to be the person in the room who remembers the lender sitting at the end of this change, trying to close a mortgage on time.

03
Delivery Quality · Finastra

Cutting sprint rework by rethinking what "ready" means

It all started when engineers kept coming back with questions mid-sprint. The stories were written. The acceptance criteria existed. But somehow, "done" kept meaning "done, but actually not quite".

Reduced sprint carry-over Fewer post-release defects 5+ cross-functional teams aligned

As Product Owner across five cross-functional teams at Finastra, a familiar pattern kept repeating: stories entered sprint, mid-sprint clarifications slowed engineers, and tickets crossed the finish line technically complete but practically incomplete. Post-release defects were the real cost.

I overhauled how we wrote user stories and acceptance criteria, not by adding more documentation, but by changing the conversation. One simple change had an outsized impact: before QA invested time writing detailed test cases, we introduced lightweight scenario reviews where Product, QA and Engineering walked through expected behaviours, edge cases and assumptions together. I introduced structured story readiness reviews with Engineering and QA before stories entered the sprint, where the three of us pressure-tested edge cases, integration assumptions, and definition of done in the same room.

The change was cultural as much as process-driven. When engineers trusted that a story was genuinely ready before they started, velocity improved. When QA had shaped the acceptance criteria rather than just checked against it, defect rates dropped.

What I learned: Backlog grooming that only involves the PM isn't grooming, it's writing. The real work happens when Engineering and QA are in the room early enough to change the story, not just read it.

04
AI Feature · Finastra

Helping customers find answers before they needed support

It all started with a support queue. Customers were raising tickets for questions the documentation already answered, they just couldn't find it. The fix wasn't more docs. It was smarter access to the ones that existed.

AI feature discovery Customer self-service

Customers were submitting support cases for answers that already existed in product documentation, the findability problem was real, and it was driving unnecessary support volume and customer frustration.

I defined and led the product requirements for an AI-driven feature designed to surface relevant support content at the moment customers needed it, contextually, within the platform experience rather than behind a separate search.

The discovery phase involved synthesising patterns across the support queue to identify the highest-frequency question clusters, then working with Product Management and Engineering to define a scoped, feasible first version that addressed the core need without overbuilding.

The initiative also fed into a broader goal of improving self-service resolution rates, reducing mean time-to-resolution for the support team, and improving the overall documentation experience.

What I learned: The most effective AI features start with an embarrassingly mundane insight, in this case, that people couldn't find the answer to a question that had already been answered. Clarity of problem beats sophistication of solution, every time.
